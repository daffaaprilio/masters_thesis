\hspace{1em}Long-read Oxford Nanopore Technology sequencing of the four accessions yielded a desirable read depth, with mean coverage across the ten largest contigs ranging from 15.29$\times$ (SBC23) to 26.18$\times$ (SBC11) (Table~\ref{tab:read-depth-summary}). These ten largest contigs correspond to the ten nuclear chromosomes of the BTx623 reference assembly, each spanning tens of megabases and together representing the chromosome-scale portion of the genome; the mitochondrial and chloroplast genomes, together with a number of small unplaced scaffolds, are considerably shorter and are therefore only captured once all contigs are considered. When depth is instead computed across all contigs, the mean coverage per sample increases modestly (17.12--30.31$\times$), but the standard deviation increases sharply relative to the nuclear-chromosome-only figures (Appendix \ref{fig:depth-sbc4} - \ref{fig:depth-sbc23}).

\section{Variant Landscape}
\hspace{1em}Variants were called for each accession against the BTx623 reference and annotated for functional consequence with SnpEff, yielding between 2.14 million (SBC23) and 2.95 million (SBC4) variants per accession (Table~\ref{tab:variant-counts}). For each variant carried by a given accession, the most severe predicted impact among its annotated effects was retained, and these were summarised into SnpEff's four impact categories: HIGH, MODERATE, LOW, and MODIFIER (Figure~\ref{fig:effect-impact-combined}). The distribution is dominated by MODIFIER-impact variants (96.3--96.4\% of calls in every accession), consistent with the expectation that most genomic variation in a whole-genome resequencing experiment falls outside coding sequence -- in intergenic, intronic, or up-/downstream regions. Variants predicted to alter protein sequence without disrupting the reading frame (LOW and MODERATE impact, e.g.\ synonymous and missense changes) each account for a further 1.6--1.8\%, while HIGH-impact variants, those predicted to severely disrupt gene function, such as stop-gains, frameshifts, or splice-site disruptions, are rare, at 0.23--0.24\% of calls.

\hspace{1em}Associating variant sites with the phenotype of interest is performed through a process called variant set intersections. For each variant record in the combined annotated VCF, the subset of accessions whose genotype carries the alternate allele (heterozygous or homozygous alternate) was determined, and records were partitioned according to this exact sample-sharing pattern into the fifteen possible non-empty subsets of the four accessions, so that a variant carried by, for example, SBC4 and SBC23 alone contributes only to that combination and not to any overlapping combination (Figure~\ref{fig:upset-plot}). Because each accession's TAA concentration and juice production phenotype is known beforehand, a given intersection can be read as a phenotype contrast: variants shared by the three TAA-high accessions (SBC4, SBC11, SBC23) but absent from the TAA-low accession (SBC10) are candidates for TAA-associated variation, etc. The size of each intersection therefore defines the starting pool of candidate variant sites carried forward into the gene-level scoring described in the following chapter.

\section{Methylation Landscape}
\hspace{1em}Genome-wide 5mC methylation levels were computed for each accession from the bedmethyl pileups, stratified by sequence context (CpG, CHG, and CHH) (Figure~\ref{fig:context-methylation-level}). All four accessions show the same characteristic plant methylation architecture, with mean methylation highest at CpG sites (53--59\%), intermediate at CHG sites (28--33\%), and lowest at CHH sites (1.2--1.5\%). This CpG $>$ CHG $\gg$ CHH ordering mirrors the distinct maintenance pathways that act on each context in plants and confirms that the modified-base calls recovered from duplex ONT sequencing reproduce the expected genome-wide methylation landscape. As with the variant impact distribution, the context-level methylation profile is broadly similar across accessions, with only modest inter-accession variation (e.g.\ CpG methylation ranging from 53.3\% in SBC10 to 58.5\% in SBC23); this baseline landscape motivates the CpG differentially methylated region (DMR) analysis presented later, which localises phenotype-associated CpG methylation changes to specific genomic regions rather than genome-wide shifts.

% \hspace{1em}Candidate variant sites for the juicy phenotype were selected under the assumption that juiciness arises from loss of function in one or more dPCD module genes. Wild-type sorghum is dry \cite{fujimotoTranscriptionalSwitchProgrammed2018}, and SBC11 is the only dry accession in the panel, so its dPCD module genes are expected to remain functional and wild-type. Variant sites found in SBC11 were therefore excluded from the selection: a loss-of-function change already present in this wild-type-like accession cannot explain why the other three accessions instead present as juicy. In practice, this was implemented by retaining only rows of the combined VCF in which SBC11's genotype does not carry the alternate allele (homozygous reference \texttt{0/0} or missing \texttt{./.}) while at least one juicy accession does, yielding 3,187,499 candidate variant sites carried forward for downstream analysis.

\hspace{1em}From the combined VCF file, variants correpond to the stem dryness and extra juiciness phenotype are each filtered out into separate phenotype-specific VCF files. Then, for each separate VCF file, each variant record was parsed and converted from one row per variant into one row per (variant, gene) pair. A single variant can carry multiple SnpEff \texttt{ANN} entries (one per overlapping transcript or gene) so these were first grouped by gene identifier; for genes with more than one candidate annotation, a single representative entry was retained using a deterministic priority: transcripts with a matching SIFT4G prediction (joined on the shared allele and transcript identifiers) were preferred, followed by the most severe impact category (HIGH $>$ MODERATE $>$ LOW $>$ MODIFIER), the original SnpEff annotation order, and finally protein-coding transcripts over other biotypes. Annotations mapping to ambiguous, fused, or multi-gene identifiers (containing a hyphen or ampersand, e.g.\ structural-variant fusion or intergenic labels) were discarded, retaining only unambiguous single-gene assignments. This reduction converts the variant-level VCF into a variant--gene table, so that a variant spanning several genes contributes an independent, appropriately prioritised annotation to each, yielding 2,180,854 variant--gene rows carried forward for extra juiciness phenotype candidates gene-level scoring, and 1,712,297 rows for stem dryness.

\hspace{1em}Each variant--gene row was then assigned a numeric score reflecting its predicted functional severity, so that genes could be ranked by their most damaging variant. SnpEff's four impact categories were mapped to equal-width quarter-bands spanning $[0,1]$ in increasing order of severity (MODIFIER: 0--0.25; LOW: 0.25--0.50; MODERATE: 0.50--0.75; HIGH: 0.75--1.00), with a variant's score defaulting to its band's midpoint. For MODERATE-impact (missense) variants with a matching SIFT4G prediction, the score was instead placed continuously within the MODERATE band in proportion to the SIFT4G damaging score, so that missense variants predicted deleterious by SIFT4G score closer to the HIGH boundary than those predicted tolerated, rather than collapsing all missense variants to the same band-midpoint value. Variant--gene rows were then collapsed to one row per gene by retaining only the single highest-scoring (most damaging) variant affecting each gene, a maximum, or worst-variant, model of gene disruption, and genes were ranked in descending order of this score.

\subsection{Enrichment test}
\hspace{1em}Each omics layer's candidate gene list was tested for enrichment of the dPCD module with a hypergeometric over-representation analysis (ORA). Given a candidate gene set of size $n$, the dPCD module, and a fixed background universe of $N = 32{,}945$ annotated sorghum genes containing $K = 66$ module genes, the test computes $P(X \geq k)$ under the hypergeometric survival function -- the probability of observing at least the $k$ module genes actually found among the candidates by chance alone -- together with the expected hit count and the fold enrichment over that expectation. The same fixed background was applied to both omics layers and to both demonstration phenotypes, so that the four tests are directly comparable (Table~\ref{tab:ora-enrichment}).

\hspace{1em}For the variant layer, the candidate set was the impact-filtered candidate gene list (score $\geq 0.5$; Table~\ref{tab:narrowing-down-steps-variant}). For the stem extra juiciness phenotype, 35 of the 66 module genes fell among the 13{,}718 variant candidates, against an expectation of 27.48, a nominal enrichment with an uncorrected p-value of 0.041 (1.27-fold enrichment). For the stem dryness phenotype, 27 module genes fell among the 11{,}356 candidates, against an expectation of 22.75 (1.19-fold, $p = 0.165$) -- consistent in direction with the extra juiciness result, but weaker.

\hspace{1em}For the DMR layer, the candidate set was the promoter-DMR-derived candidate gene list (Table~\ref{tab:narrowing-down-steps-dmr}). For stem extra juiciness, 4 of the 66 module genes were recovered among the 1{,}530 DMR candidates, against an expectation of 3.07 (1.31-fold, $p = 0.368$); for stem dryness, 12 module genes fell among the 5{,}367 candidates, against an expectation of 10.75 (1.12-fold, $p = 0.388$). Neither methylation-derived candidate list shows appreciable enrichment of the dPCD module. Overall, only the variant layer for the stem extra juiciness phenotype shows a nominal enrichment (uncorrected $p = 0.041$, from four tests without multiple-testing correction), while the remaining three tests are enriched in direction only.

\section{Application of the platform -- TAA metabolism genes}
\hspace{1em}The platform is next applied to the TAA concentration phenotype. Unlike the stem juiciness demonstration, no gene of the TAA biosynthesis pathway has been identified in any plant species, so no literature-curated gene module exists to serve as the known-gene set. The over-representation analysis used to evaluate the demonstration (Table~\ref{tab:ora-enrichment}) therefore cannot be applied here, and the variant and DMR layers instead act as hypothesis-generating filters that narrow the genome down to a manageable set of genes potentially involved in TAA metabolism. The coexpression layer is likewise redesigned: rather than querying a known module, it ranks isomerase-family genes by their coexpression with genes known to be upregulated by phytochrome, following the hypothesis that aconitate isomerase expression is light-regulated.

\subsection{Variant analysis}
\hspace{1em}SBC10 is the only accession in the panel with a low TAA concentration in its stem juice, while the reference strain BTx623 is assumed to carry an average TAA concentration. Candidate variant sites for the low TAA production trait were therefore defined as those private to SBC10: carrying the alternate allele in SBC10 while the three high-TAA accessions retain the reference allele (Table~\ref{tab:taa-candidate-patterns}). This selection follows the assumption that a loss of function occurring in SBC10 alone, in one or more genes of the TAA biosynthesis pathway, gives rise to its low-TAA phenotype. Because only one accession carries the contrasting phenotype, a single sharing pattern qualifies, in contrast to the seven patterns admitted by the stem extra juiciness contrast (Table~\ref{tab:candidate-patterns}).

\hspace{1em}The SBC10-private variants were converted into variant--gene rows, scored, and collapsed to one row per gene using exactly the same procedure as the demonstration phenotypes: the SnpEff \texttt{ANN} entries of each variant were grouped by gene and reduced to a single representative annotation per gene, each variant--gene row was assigned a severity score in the impact band of its predicted effect (refined by the SIFT4G damaging score for missense variants), and each gene retained the score of its most damaging variant (Figure~\ref{fig:taa-variant-gene-score}). Applying the same impact score threshold of 0.5 narrowed 327,868 phenotype-associated variant sites, spread over 21,928 genes, down to 3,032 candidate genes for the low TAA production trait (Table~\ref{tab:narrowing-down-steps-variant-taa}). This candidate list is roughly a quarter the size of the stem extra juiciness list (13,718 genes), reflecting the stricter single-accession pattern used here.

\subsection{Differentially methylated region analysis}
\hspace{1em}Candidate genes from the methylation layer were selected following the same promoter-methylation logic as the demonstration phenotypes, contrasting the low-TAA accession SBC10 against each of the three high-TAA accessions. Under the working assumption that promoter CpG methylation indicates transcriptional repression, a gene is a candidate for the low TAA production trait when its promoter is hypermethylated in SBC10 relative to a high-TAA accession, since repression of a TAA biosynthesis gene in SBC10 alone would be consistent with its reduced TAA concentration. The three pairwise comparisons satisfying this direction (Table~\ref{tab:taa-dmr-comparison-matrix}) yielded 3,091 promoter DMRs, which converted into 2,617 candidate genes (Table~\ref{tab:narrowing-down-steps-dmr-taa}).

\subsection{Gene coexpression analysis}
\hspace{1em}No transcription factor or gene module is known to regulate the TAA biosynthesis pathway, so the coexpression layer cannot be queried with a curated module as it was in the demonstration. Instead, the query set was built from the light-regulation of aconitate isomerase. Eprintsev et al. \cite{eprintsevAconitateIsomeraseMaize2021} reported that aconitate isomerase expression in maize is induced by white and by red light, indicating that its expression is regulated by phytochrome, while a set of \textit{A. thaliana} respiratory enzymes whose expression is likewise induced by light and upregulated by phytochrome is already documented \cite{igamberdievMitochondriaPhotosyntheticCells2023}. Three of these \textit{Arabidopsis} genes have an identifiable \textit{Sorghum bicolor} ortholog and together form the phytochrome-upregulated gene module used here as bait genes (Appendix~\ref{tab:phy-module}): an alternative NAD(P)H-ubiquinone oxidoreductase (EGI: 8080027), the circadian clock regulator protein CCA1 (EGI: 8080836), and heat shock protein 82 (EGI: 8057342).

\hspace{1em}TAA is converted from \textit{cis}-aconitate by an as yet unidentified aconitate isomerase. The target set was therefore defined as every isomerase gene annotated in the sorghum genome by GO annotation, 461 genes in total, reflecting the hypothesis that the aconitate isomerase is among them. Each target gene was then scored by its z-transformed coexpression against each bait gene, and the isomerase most strongly coexpressed with each bait gene was taken as that bait gene's aconitate isomerase candidate (Table~\ref{tab:taa-coexpression-ranking}). The strongest relationship is between heat shock protein 82 and a 70 kDa peptidyl-prolyl isomerase (EGI: 8072909, $z = 8.88$), followed by CCA1 with an inositol-3-phosphate synthase (EGI: 110432775, $z = 5.25$) and the NAD(P)H-ubiquinone oxidoreductase with a putative glucose-6-phosphate 1-epimerase (EGI: 8075824, $z = 4.36$).

\hspace{1em}Unlike the demonstration, these candidates cannot be validated, as no gene of the TAA biosynthesis pathway is known in any plant species. They are therefore hypotheses to be tested experimentally rather than confirmed candidates. It is also worth noting that the candidates are nominated purely from GO annotation, and none of them carries an isomerase annotation specific to the TCA cycle or to organic-acid metabolism; some, such as the putative glucose-6-phosphate 1-epimerase, are only tentatively annotated. This is consistent with aconitate isomerase remaining unidentified in the sorghum genome, and indeed in any plant genome. Taken together, the three layers narrow the genome down to 3,032 genes carrying a high-impact SBC10-private variant, 2,617 genes with a promoter hypermethylated in SBC10, and a short list of isomerase genes coexpressed with phytochrome-upregulated genes, providing evidence-based starting points for the identification of the TAA biosynthesis pathway.
