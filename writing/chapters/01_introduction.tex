\chapter{Introduction}

% sorghum intro
\hspace{1em}Sorghum (\textit{Sorghum bicolor} L. Moench) is one of the most widely cultivated cereal crops in the world, ranking fifth in global production after maize, rice, wheat, and barley \cite{silvaProgressChallengesSorghum2022a}. It is a type of grass, attributing its classification in the Poaceae family, along with the other crops species mentioned previously: maize (\textit{Zea mays}), rice (\textit{Oryza sativa}), wheat (\textit{Triticum aestivum}), and barley (\textit{Hordeum vulgare}). Sorghum and the other cereal crops collectively are considered as distant relatives to the model species \textit{Arabidopsis thaliana}, in which they are assigned within a monophyletic clade of Angiospermae (flowering plants). 
% sorghum produces TAA 
Apart from being an agriculturally important plant, sorghum is also known for producing valuable secondary metabolites, such as phenolic compounds \cite{chibrikovSweetSorghumJuice2024} and organic acids including aconitic acid \cite{bruniAconiticAcidRecovery2022a}.

% what is TAA
\hspace{1em}In nature, aconitic acid (propene-1,2,3-tricarboxylic acid) can exist as two forms: \textit{cis} and \textit{trans}, in which the \textit{trans}-form is more stable and energetically more favourable \cite{bruniAconiticAcidRecovery2022a} \cite{igamberdievOrganicAcidsPools2016}. Aconitic acid, in its \textit{trans-} form, TAA, can be utilized for various industrial purpose, including as an alternative to synthetic plasticizer. 
% plasticizer
Plasticizer is commonly used in plastic manufacturing as an additive to the polymer to enhance the flexibility of the final product. The most commonly used plasticizer is made from synthetic compound, such as di(2-ethylhexyl)phthalate (DEHP), a phthalic acid ester (PAE) that, despite its widespread industrial use, is recognised as chemically hazardous. Naturally occurring TAA has been proposed as a bio-based precursor for plasticizer synthesis, offering a sustainable alternative to synthetic PAEs \cite{bruniAconiticAcidRecovery2022a}.

% other sources of TAA: but sorghum is still better hehe
\hspace{1em}Other than sorghum, the natural accumulation of TAA is also reported in sugarcane (\textit{Saccharum officinarum}) \cite{bruniAconiticAcidRecovery2022a}. In both sugarcane and sorghum, TAA is extracted from the stem juice, obtained by squeezing the stem after stripping of the leaves, panicles, and peduncle \cite{chibrikovSweetSorghumJuice2024}. Between the two, sorghum presents a more ideal source for TAA, as in sorghum, the stem is largely regarded as a waste material, and the extraction of TAA or other compounds would not compete with its primary agricultural commodity, the grain. In contrast, extracting TAA from sugarcane stem juice would compete directly with the plant's primary commodity, sucrose, given that the same stem juice is the feedstock for sugar production. Extraction of TAA through biological engineering is also possible, i.e., through the genetic modification of an \textit{Escherichia coli} strain, at the expense of the need for a controlled laboratory environment and expensive feedstock \cite{guoEfficientTransaconiticAcid2026}. This suggests that sorghum is the most ideal source of natural TAA.

% research context and objective
\hspace{1em}In the present study, we aim to develop a platform to identify the genetic basis of metabolic variation in \textit{Sorghum bicolor}. To achieve this, we performed and integrated multi-omics analysis, covering the genomics, epigenomics, and transcriptomics layers, enabling us to link genetic variations and regulations underlying the changes in phenotypes. Then, we demonstrate the platform's utility using the stem juiciness trait as a proof-of-concept phenotype. Finally, we apply the platform to identify candidate genes involved in TAA metabolism, and as an attempt to unravel the genetic basis of the variation in TAA production.

% \hspace{1em}TAA can be produced through microbial biosynthesis in metabolically engineered strains \cite{guo_efficient_2026}, or extracted directly from plant biomass, primarily from the stem juice of sugarcane and sweet sorghum \cite{bruni_aconitic_2022}. Among plant-based sources, sorghum is considered the more suitable feedstock on practical grounds. Sugarcane stem juice simultaneously serves as the principal substrate for refined sugar production and as an industrial fermentation feedstock, creating an inherent conflict between food and non-food uses. Sorghum presents no such constraint: its grain is harvested for food, while its stem, which carries no comparable nutritional value can be directed toward TAA extraction without competing with the food supply. This functional separation of plant parts positions sorghum as a practical and sustainable feedstock for TAA production.

% % Role of TAA in plant physiology
% % \subsection{Role of TAA in plant physiology}
% \hspace{1em}Among the documented physiological roles of TAA in plants is its function as a stable secondary carbon pool derived from the TCA cycle. Under active photosynthesis, the TCA cycle operates as an open, non-cyclic structure rather than a closed loop, leading to the accumulation of organic acid intermediates, including TAA, that would otherwise be rapidly consumed \cite{igamberdiev_organic_2016, igamberdiev_citrate_2020, igamberdiev_mitochondria_2023}. TAA itself arises from cis-aconitate, a TCA cycle intermediate, via isomerization to its chemically stable trans-form \cite{igamberdiev_organic_2016}. Its stability allows it to accumulate to significant concentrations in plant tissues, and its biosynthesis has been shown to be coupled to light availability, suggesting a functional link between TAA metabolism and high photosynthetic activity \cite{igamberdiev_organic_2016}.

% \hspace{1em}Additionally, TAA also functions as a chemical defense compound in plants. In wheat, TAA serves as a precursor to methylated trans-aconitate derivatives that accumulate at sites of fungal penetration by \textit{Blumeria graminis} and exhibit potent antifungal activity, with their production further amplified by silicon treatment, consistent with a phytoalexin-like role. The conversion of TAA to its antifungal methylated forms is proposed to be catalyzed by a trans-aconitate \textit{O}-methyltransferase \cite{remus-borel_aconitate_2009}; however, no such enzyme has been characterized in plants to date. The silicon-amplified accumulation of methyl-TAA further suggests that this methylation step is subject to stress-inducible regulation, adding a layer of enzymatic control to the defense response \cite{remus-borel_aconitate_2009}. Against insect herbivores, TAA stored in the non-phloem tissues of barnyard grass (\textit{Echinochloa crus-galli}) deters feeding by the brown planthopper \textit{Nilaparvata lugens}: upon probing through parenchyma cells, the insect encounters TAA and withdraws its stylets before reaching the phloem, effectively preventing nutrient ingestion \cite{hattori_probing_2001}. TAA also exerts allelopathic effects in the soil environment. When released by TAA-accumulating grasses into rotation systems, it is absorbed by neighboring plant roots, where it induces reactive oxygen species overproduction, increases membrane permeability, impairs water and nutrient uptake, and ultimately suppresses growth \cite{bortolo_trans-aconitic_2018}.

% \section{Research context}
% \hspace{1em}Despite the established roles of TAA in plant metabolism and defense, the genes encoding the enzymes responsible for its biosynthesis remain unidentified in any plant species. The primary biosynthetic enzyme, aconitate isomerase (EC 5.3.3.7), has been detected and partially purified in maize. Primers designed from conserved regions of known bacterial and fungal aconitate isomerase sequences were used to detect the enzyme in the cytosol of the leaves \cite{eprintsev_aconitate_2021}. Expression was found to be light-dependent and responsive to red light, suggesting regulation by the phytochrome system. This is consistent with the broader link between TAA accumulation and photosynthetic activity \cite{eprintsev_aconitate_2021, igamberdiev_organic_2016}. Despite this progress, the responsible gene was not concretely reported in the paper.

% % \hspace{1em}Two additional gaps remain in connecting TAA to its defense functions. First, since TAA is produced as a photosynthetic overflow metabolite, leaves are the likely primary site of biosynthesis; yet no transporter has been described that moves TAA to storage sites such as pith parenchyma vacuoles, nor has the accumulation of TAA specifically in non-phloem tissues of barnyard grass been attributed to any known carrier. Second, \cite{remus-borel_aconitate_2009} showed that TAA must be methylated to function as an antifungal phytoalexin in wheat \cite{remus-borel_aconitate_2009}, but the plant enzyme responsible for this conversion has not been identified and its expression pattern under pathogen stress is unknown.

% \section{Methods Overview}
% \hspace{1em}The platform integrates three omics layers as independent, parallel sources of evidence. The two layers, genomics and epigenomics, are anchored on the same four \textit{Sorghum bicolor} accessions of contrasting phenotypes, sequenced with Oxford Nanopore (ONT) long-read sequencing. While the transcriptomic layer, particularly gene co-expression analysis, is derived from publicly available sorghum RNA-seq data processed under the ATTED-II framework \cite{obayashi_atted-ii_2022}. 

% \hspace{1em}At the genomic layer, variants are called for each accession against the reference genome and annotated for functional consequence, further prioritising amino-acid-altering variants predicted to be deleterious. Candidate causative genes are identified as those carrying variants shared by the accessions of one phenotypic class but absent from the contrasting accession(s). At the epigenomic layer, DNA methylation is profiled for each accession and used to call differentially methylated regions (DMRs) between phenotypically contrasting accessions. DMRs are overlapped with annotated gene features, and genes whose promoter methylation is consistently altered in the direction expected of transcriptional repression across the phenotypic contrasts form a second, independent list of candidate causative genes. A list of candidate causative genes list for the associated phenotype is the product of either genomic and epigenomic layer. At the transcriptomics layer, the gene co-expression analysis data is used differently in each case study, reflecting how much is already known about the underlying gene. 

% \hspace{1em}For the positive control, we adapt the coexpression-based transcription factor discovery strategy of Hirai et al.\ (2007), who identified the regulatory transcription factors Myb28 and Myb29 not by direct functional annotation but by their consistent clustering with a known set of pathway structural genes across both condition-independent (public microarray compendium) and condition-specific coexpression networks. We apply the same logic in reverse: rather than starting from a single bait gene, we use the dPCD marker gene set (Olvera-Carrillo et al., 2015) as the query set to construct a coexpression module, on the expectation that the D-gene transcription factor will emerge as the transcription factor most consistently co-expressed with this query set, mirroring how Myb28 and Myb29 emerged from clustering with aliphatic glucosinolate biosynthetic genes rather than from prior functional characterization.

% \hspace{1em}For the TAA narrative, we use sorghum RNA-seq-based gene co-expression data to rank every gene annotated with isomerase activity by its co-expression with a small proxy module of light-responsive genes, reflecting evidence that aconitate isomerase expression is regulated by phytochrome \cite{eprintsev_aconitate_2021, igamberdiev_phytochromemediated_2014}.

Candidate causative genes are identified as those carrying variants shared by the accessions of one phenotypic class but absent from the contrasting accession(s).

DMRs are overlapped with annotated gene features, and genes whose promoter methylation is consistently altered in the direction expected of transcriptional repression across the phenotypic contrasts form a second, independent list of candidate causative genes.

% % \hspace{1em}At the transcriptomic layer, gene co-expression relationships are derived from publicly available sorghum expression data following the ATTED-II framework, but the layer is used differently in each case study, reflecting how much is already known about the underlying gene. For the stem-juiciness positive control, the sorghum homologs of the known dPCD executor genes serve as bait genes: a co-expression network is built around them in search of a cluster that also contains the D-gene transcription factor, testing whether the transcriptomic layer alone can recover a known regulator from its downstream targets. For the TAA analysis, no equivalent bait module exists, since the causative gene itself is unknown; instead, every isomerase-family gene annotated in the sorghum genome is ranked by its co-expression with genes regulated by phytochrome, reflecting the reported light-dependent regulation of aconitate isomerase activity \cite{eprintsev_aconitate_2021}, nominating the top-ranked isomerase genes as aconitate isomerase candidates.

% % \hspace{1em}For the stem-juiciness positive control, the dPCD executor module additionally serves as a reference set against which the variant- and DMR-derived candidate lists are tested for enrichment, assessing whether either layer alone recovers components of the known pathway. For the TAA analysis, the variant-, DMR-, and co-expression-derived candidate lists together constitute the prioritised candidates for aconitate isomerase.
